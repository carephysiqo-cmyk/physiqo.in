Why lower back pain is so common

Lower back pain affects 8 out of 10 people at some point in their lives. In India, it is one of the leading causes of missed workdays, especially among office workers in cities like Noida, Delhi, and Mumbai who spend long hours sitting at desks.

The good news? Most lower back pain is not caused by serious structural damage. It is often the result of weak core muscles, tight hip flexors, poor posture, and long periods of inactivity. This means targeted exercises can make a significant difference — often within days.

The 5 exercises below are recommended by physiotherapists for most common types of lower back pain. They are gentle, safe for beginners, and require no equipment — just a yoga mat or a firm surface.

1

Knee-to-Chest Stretch

Beginner
30 sec
Hold
3 sets
Each side
Daily
Frequency
Best for: Morning stiffness, tight hip flexors, disc-related lower back pain

How to do it

  1. 1Lie flat on your back on a firm surface with your knees bent and feet flat on the floor.
  2. 2Gently pull your right knee toward your chest using both hands. Keep your left leg flat or slightly bent.
  3. 3Hold for 20–30 seconds. You should feel a gentle stretch in your lower back and glutes — not pain.
  4. 4Slowly lower your leg and repeat with the left knee. Then try pulling both knees to your chest together.
Avoid if: You feel sharp or shooting pain radiating down your leg during this stretch.
2

Cat-Cow Stretch

Beginner
10 reps
Per set
3 sets
Total
Twice
Per day
Best for: Spinal stiffness, poor posture, muscle spasms, office workers

How to do it

  1. 1Start on all fours — hands directly under shoulders, knees under hips. Keep your spine neutral.
  2. 2Cat: Breathe out and round your back upward toward the ceiling, tucking your chin to your chest and your tailbone under. Hold 3 seconds.
  3. 3Cow: Breathe in and let your belly drop toward the floor, lifting your head and tailbone upward. Hold 3 seconds.
  4. 4Flow smoothly between the two positions for 10 repetitions. Move slowly and with your breath.
Pro tip: This is an excellent exercise to do first thing in the morning before getting out of bed, or after long hours of sitting.
3

Pelvic Tilts

Beginner
15 reps
Per set
3 sets
Total
Daily
Frequency
Best for: Weak core muscles, lumbar lordosis, post-pregnancy back pain

How to do it

  1. 1Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor, and arms by your sides.
  2. 2Gently tighten your abdominal muscles and flatten your lower back against the floor by tilting your pelvis slightly.
  3. 3Hold this position for 5 seconds while breathing normally.
  4. 4Relax and repeat. The movement is small — focus on engaging your core, not lifting your hips.
Pro tip: Pelvic tilts are the foundation of core stability. Master this before moving to more advanced exercises like planks or crunches.
4

Bird-Dog

Intermediate
10 sec
Hold
10 reps
Each side
Daily
Frequency
Best for: Core stability, spine alignment, preventing back pain recurrence

How to do it

  1. 1Start on all fours with your back flat and core gently engaged. Do not let your back arch or round.
  2. 2Slowly extend your right arm straight forward and your left leg straight back at the same time. Keep both parallel to the floor.
  3. 3Hold for 5–10 seconds while keeping your hips level. Avoid rotating or tilting to one side.
  4. 4Return to the starting position and switch sides (left arm + right leg). That's one rep.
Common mistake: Raising the leg too high and arching the lower back. Keep movements slow and controlled — the goal is stability, not height.
5

Glute Bridge

Beginner–Intermediate
15 reps
Per set
3 sets
Total
Daily
Frequency
Best for: Weak glutes, SI joint pain, general lower back strengthening

How to do it

  1. 1Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart, arms relaxed at your sides.
  2. 2Press your feet into the floor, squeeze your glutes, and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
  3. 3Hold at the top for 2–3 seconds. Do not hyperextend your lower back — keep the movement controlled.
  4. 4Slowly lower your hips back to the floor and repeat.
Pro tip: Weak glutes are one of the most overlooked causes of lower back pain. This single exercise can make a remarkable difference within 2–3 weeks.

Tips for best results

🕐 Be consistent

Do these exercises daily for at least 2 weeks. One session will not fix chronic pain — consistency is what creates lasting change.

🧘 Start slow

Start with fewer repetitions and gradually increase. Pain during exercise is a warning sign — stop immediately if you feel sharp pain.

🌡️ Warm up first

A 5-minute warm walk or gentle movement before exercising reduces the risk of injury and helps muscles respond better.

💧 Stay hydrated

Spinal discs need adequate hydration to function well. Drink at least 2–3 litres of water daily, especially in Indian summers.

🪑 Fix your posture

Exercises alone won't help if you sit hunched for 8 hours. Set a timer to stand and walk for 2 minutes every 45 minutes.

😴 Rest matters too

Sleep on a firm mattress, preferably on your side with a pillow between your knees. Avoid sleeping on your stomach.

When to see a physiotherapist

While these exercises help most common back pain, some situations require professional assessment. See a physiotherapist if:

  • → Pain has lasted more than 2 weeks despite home exercises
  • → Pain radiates down your leg (sciatica)
  • → You experience numbness or tingling in your legs or feet
  • → Pain worsens at night or is not relieved by rest
  • → You have had a recent injury, fall, or accident
  • → Pain is accompanied by fever or unexplained weight loss

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take for these exercises to work? +

Most people notice improvement within 2–4 weeks of consistent daily practice. Acute pain may reduce in 3–7 days with proper exercises and rest. Chronic pain typically takes 4–8 weeks of consistent effort.

Should I exercise if my lower back hurts? +

Gentle movement is usually better than complete rest for most types of back pain. However, avoid exercises that increase pain. If pain is severe, consult a physiotherapist first.

How many times a day should I do these exercises? +

Most physiotherapists recommend doing these exercises once or twice daily. The morning session helps reduce stiffness; an evening session helps relax muscles after a day of work.

Can I do these exercises during pregnancy? +

Some of these exercises, like pelvic tilts and cat-cow, are commonly recommended during pregnancy. However, always consult your doctor or a women's health physiotherapist before starting any exercise programme during pregnancy.
